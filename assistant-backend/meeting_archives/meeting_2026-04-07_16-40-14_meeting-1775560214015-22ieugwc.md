### 1. Core Strategy  
I'd start with a shadow rollout, routing a small percentage of live traffic to the new AI endpoint while keeping the old one active. I’d log every request and response side‑by‑side so we can compare outputs without affecting users. After a week of data, I’d run a quick A/B test to confirm the new model meets safety and performance thresholds. Finally, I’d roll out the feature incrementally, monitoring telemetry and user feedback before full deployment.  

### 2. Explicit Metrics (The Telemetry)  
* **Safety Flag Rate:** How often the model triggers a content‑filter or safety warning, indicating potential unsafe outputs.  
* **Confidence Score Distribution:** The average confidence of the model’s predictions, helping spot over‑confident or uncertain responses.  
* **Latency (ms):** End‑to‑end response time to ensure the new model doesn’t degrade user experience.  
* **Error Rate (%):** Proportion of requests that fail or return malformed responses, signaling stability issues.  

### 3. Implicit Metrics (User Behavior)  
* **Engagement Time:** Average time users spend interacting with the AI feature, showing whether it’s useful or frustrating.  
* **Repeat Usage Rate:** How often users return to the feature, indicating satisfaction and trust.  
* **Support Ticket Volume:** Number of help‑desk tickets mentioning the AI feature, flagging potential usability or safety concerns.  

### 4. Edge Cases & Risks  
One risk is that the shadow traffic may not fully represent edge user scenarios, so we might miss rare failure modes. Another pitfall is that safety flags could be too aggressive, causing legitimate content to be blocked and hurting user trust. Finally, if the new model’s latency spikes under load, it could lead to timeouts and a degraded experience that users will notice.
